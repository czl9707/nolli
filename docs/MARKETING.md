# Topic 1: Why this map exists

Standard maps treat Mies' Barcelona Pavilion the same as the parking garage next door. Same dot. Same zoom. Same everything.

As an architect, this always bothered me. Great buildings deserve more than a pin.

So I'm building a map that understands the difference. One that shows figure-ground like Noli drew Rome in 1748. One that pins up drawings, photos, and notes the way studios do.

For people who see buildings, not just buildings.

---
Google Maps knows where every building is.

It has no idea which ones matter.

I spent 7 years studying architecture. Now I code. This is the thing I always wished existed — a map that treats masterpieces like masterpieces.

Heavy textures. Figure-ground clarity. Pin-up boards for every building. Not a red dot on a beige grid.

---
Architects don't navigate by address. They navigate by building.

"Where's the Gehry?" "Two blocks past the Moneo." That's how the conversation goes.

But no map speaks that language. Until now.

Building a map for people who look up.

# Topic 2: The Noli map idea

In 1748, Giovanni Nolli mapped Rome. He didn't draw streets — he drew solids and voids. Public space was light. Private was dark. The map revealed what walking the city felt like.

278 years later, most maps still don't do this.

This one does.

---
Noli's map of Rome is still hanging in architecture studios after 278 years. Not because it was accurate. Because it showed something no other map could — the relationship between space and form.

I applied the same principle. Every building rendered in figure-ground. Heavy texture. Clear edges. You don't just see where things are. You see what they are.

---
There's a reason architecture schools still teach Noli.

His map didn't just show Rome. It made you understand Rome. The way solid meets void. The way public space flows between buildings.

I wanted a map that does that for architecture everywhere. Not satellite view. Not a flat grid. Figure-ground, the way architects draw.

# Topic 3: The pin-up culture

---
Every architect knows the pin-up.

You pin your drawings on the board. Photos next to them. Sketches. Notes from the critic. Everything about one project, in one place, at one glance.

I put that on a map. Click any building. The map folds into a pin-up board. Drawings. Photos. Context. The whole story.

---
Architecture education is basically this: put your work on the wall, stand next to it, get roasted.

The pin-up board is where buildings get understood. Not in isolation — surrounded by context, drawings, competing ideas.

I digitized that. Every building on the map unfolds into its own pin-up. The studio critique, now on a map.

---
The best part of architecture school wasn't the lectures. It was the pin-up.

Your project on the wall. Plans, sections, model photos, sketchy notes from midnight. Everything pinned together. That board told the whole story.

I built a map that does that for every building. Click. Animate. Pin up.
